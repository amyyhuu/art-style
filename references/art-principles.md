# Art Principles Reference

Use this file only when a request needs deeper art direction, critique, or prompt craft. For this skill, prefer a cute, pink, polished aesthetic by default unless the user asks for another direction.

## Composition

- Start with the focal point: subject contrast, placement, scale, edge sharpness, or light should tell the eye where to land first.
- Check the big shape before details. A strong silhouette, value mass, or path of movement matters more than decorative texture.
- Use hierarchy: primary, secondary, and quiet areas. If every part is equally loud, the image becomes flat.
- Balance symmetry with interruption. Perfect symmetry can feel iconic; asymmetry can feel alive.
- Use negative space as an active shape, especially in posters, covers, editorial illustration, and gallery work.

## Value And Light

- A readable value plan should work in grayscale before color is added.
- Decide whether the piece is high key, low key, or high contrast.
- Use light direction to support the story: frontal light feels clear, side light adds form, backlight adds silhouette and mood.
- Keep cast shadows consistent with the light source.

## Color

- Name the palette by role, not only hue: dominant field, accent, neutral, temperature contrast, emotional note.
- Use a limited palette when the concept needs clarity or graphic force.
- Use complementary accents sparingly for emphasis.
- Watch color temperature in shadows; cool shadows and warm lights are common, but not mandatory.
- For cultural motifs, avoid treating color as decoration only. Ask what the color signifies in that context.

### Cute Pink Palette Defaults

- Foundation: blush pink, rose milk, cream, warm white, pearl, and pale peach.
- Contrast: cherry red, raspberry, cocoa brown, ink black, or deep plum in small amounts.
- Shadow notes: lavender gray, mauve, dusty lilac, or warm beige.
- Fresh accents: mint, butter yellow, sky blue, or soft green, used sparingly so the palette does not become one-note.
- Finish: glossy highlights, satin sheen, pearly surfaces, soft paper grain, or tiny star-like sparkle when the brief calls for charm.

## Medium And Surface

- For oil or acrylic: mention brush scale, impasto, glazing, underpainting, or visible canvas.
- For watercolor: mention paper grain, transparent washes, blooms, drybrush, and preserved highlights.
- For ink: mention line weight, hatching, wash, edge discipline, and negative space.
- For digital painting: mention layer feel, brush texture, edge control, lighting, and finish level.
- For print/poster work: mention flat shapes, registration texture, halftone, risograph grain, screenprint ink, or lithographic softness.

## Critique Checklist

1. Intent: what appears to be the goal?
2. Readability: can the main subject be understood quickly?
3. Composition: where does the eye go first, second, third?
4. Value: does the image hold together in grayscale?
5. Color: does the palette support mood and hierarchy?
6. Edges: are hard and soft edges used intentionally?
7. Anatomy or perspective: are distortions intentional or distracting?
8. Material: do surfaces behave believably?
9. Context: does the work respect cultural, historical, or subject matter cues?
10. Next move: what one change would most improve the piece?

## Prompt Templates

### Concept Art

```text
Create cute pink aesthetic concept art of [subject] in [setting], with [mood].
Composition: [framing, focal point, scale relationships].
Lighting: [source, time, contrast].
Palette: blush pink, cream, pearl, [accent], [shadow color].
Medium: [broad medium or finish].
Details: [3-5 visual specifics, including charming small details].
Avoid: watermark, logo, extra text, clutter, unintended style imitation.
```

### Poster Or Cover

```text
Create a cute pink aesthetic print-ready poster/cover for [topic].
Text (verbatim): "[exact words]"
Composition: strong central image, clear title hierarchy, safe margins.
Visual language: [graphic, painterly, collage, photographic, etc.] with polished soft charm.
Palette: blush pink, cream, cherry accent, and one balancing cool accent.
Constraints: readable typography, no fake logos, no extra text.
```

## Style And Copyright

- Do not ask for an image "in the style of" a living artist. Use broad movement, medium, era, or visual traits instead.
- Public-domain artists and historical movements can be referenced, but still translate the reference into visual traits when possible.
- For copyrighted characters or brands, follow the user's rights and safety context. Prefer original alternatives when the request is for new commercial artwork.
- For museum or art-history facts that may have changed, verify with reliable sources.
